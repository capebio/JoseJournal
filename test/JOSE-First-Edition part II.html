From the field

Every edition begins in a notebook.

A single timed visit — a bee on a Hermannia at 13h40 — is, in JOSE, a citable record: it carries its own provenance, its observer, and a locality held at quarter-degree resolution so a rare plant is never given away.

The field note is not the rough draft that gets discarded once the paper is written. It is the first version of the living treatment — and it stays, attributable, for as long as the record lives.

Founding statement

A journal that does not close when the science keeps moving.

Science does not stand still, and neither should the article. JOSE is built for a simple, stubborn idea: that a record of the living world should be able to grow, be corrected, and stay traceable — without ever losing what was once said.

A conventional journal publishes a snapshot and withdraws. We keep the snapshot — frozen, dated, citable, nomenclaturally sound — and let the living treatment around it accrete: new specimens, refined descriptions, pollinator observations, corrected localities, dissenting opinions, each carrying its own provenance. Nothing is unattributed; nothing is silently rewritten.

From the smallest citable field note to a full revision, every contribution is a knowledge object — connected to the specimens, sequences, places, and people behind it. Sensitive localities are generalised by default and disclosed only under governed, expiring access. The work is federated rather than owned, and it is Diamond Open Access: free to read, and free to publish.

This first edition is a beginning, not a monument. It is published from the southern tip of Africa, in the conviction that a researcher on a slow connection should hold the same materials as one in any wealthy institution — and that the living world is best kept legible by keeping it open.

— The Founding Editors · Journal of Systematics & Ecology · Volume I, MMXXVI
